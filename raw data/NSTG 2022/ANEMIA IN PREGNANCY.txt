ANEMIA IN PREGNANCY 
Introduction
Anaemia is the most common complication of pregnancy in sub-Saharan Africa. It is a diminution below normal of the total circulating haemoglobin mass. World Health Organization definition of anaemia. Haemoglobin concentration less than 11 g/dL or a haematocrit less than 33% in peripheral blood
For practical purposes in developing and tropical countries, a haemoglobin concentration of 10 g/dL or haematocrit of 30% is taken as the lowest cut off limit. Below these levels there may be adverse foetal and maternal outcomes
Aetiology
- Physiological - this is as a result of haemo-dilution of pregnancy; where the increase in red cell mass is proportionally less than the plasma increase.
- Pathological - which can further be classified as:
  - Nutritional deficiency: Iron, Folic acid, Vitamin B12
  - Hemorrhagic:
    - Overt - APH, PPH, Miscarriages
    - Occult – Intestinal helminthiasis
  - Hemolytic Anaemia
  - Hereditary: Thalassemia, Sickle
  - Acquired: Infections, Malaria, TB, UTI
  - Bone Marrow Insufficiency: due to neoplasm, chronic kidney disease, etc.

Classification – based on severity
- Mild - PCV 25 - 29%
- Moderate - PCV 20 - 24%
- Severe PCV < 20%

CLINICAL FEATURES OF ANEMIA IN PREGNANCY 
Clinical Features
- May be asymptomatic
- Generalised weakness, lassitude and easy fatiguability
- Headaches
- Dyspnea on mild exertion
- Ankle swelling
- Pallor
■ Jaundice may or may not be present  
■ Pedal oedema  
■ Tachypnea  
■ Tachycardia and Haemic murmurs  
■ Hepatomegaly: not invariable  
■ Splenomegaly: not invariable  
· Anaemic heart failure in extreme cases  

COMPLICATIONS OF ANEMIA IN PREGNANCY 
Complications  
· Maternal Complications:  
  · Abortion  
  · Cardiac failure  
  · Reduced ability to tolerate blood loss at delivery  
  · Reduced ability to tolerate anaesthesia  
  · Diminished resistance to infection  
  · Preterm labour.  
  · Precipitate labour may occur  
  · Death  
· Foetal Complications:  
  · Spontaneous abortion  
  · Intrauterine growth restriction  
  · Intrauterine foetal death (leading to still birth)  
  · Prematurity  
  · Risk of developing anaemia within 2 - 3 months of birth if mother suffered iron deficiency anaemia  

INVESIGATIONS OF ANEMIA IN PREGNANCY 
Investigations  
■ FBC and peripheral blood film  
■ Midstream urine: M/C/S  
■ Renal function test  
■ Stool analysis: ova, cysts, parasites, occult blood  
■ Blood group and cross-match blood  
■ Haemoglobin genotype  
■ VDRL  
■ HIV screening.  
■ Ultrasound scan (e.g. of abdomen, pelvis).  
■ Bone marrow biopsy if bone marrow involvement is suspected  

TREATMENT OF ANEMIA IN PREGNANCY 
Treatment Goals  
■ Correct haematocrit.  
■ Treat underlying cause(s) (see causes of anaemia above)  
■ Foetal surveillance: commonly ultrasound, cardiotocography, foetal kick chart etc.  

Correction of haematocrit:  
■ Oral haematinics  
  · For mild and moderate anaemia - Ferrous sulphate 200 mg daily and folic acid 5 mg daily Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) 100 mg three times daily.

Prevention

Counselling on contraception; adequate spacing of pregnancies.
Malaria prophylaxis in pregnancy.
Chemoprophylaxis against helminthiasis.
Prompt and appropriate treatment of febrile illnesses in pregnancy
Improvement of the socioeconomic status of the people
