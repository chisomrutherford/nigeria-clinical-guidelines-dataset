CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS OF ASPHYXIATED BABIES

(This depends on the severity and degree of hypoxic - Ischaemic encephalopathy)
■ Inability to cry or suck,
■ Global hypotonia and poor activity or global hypertonia with brisk deep tendon reflexes
■ Shock and oliguria
■ Poor temperature regulation,
■ Paralytic ileus,
■ Respiratory distress,
■ Bleeding tendencies,
■ Seizures

TREATMENT OF ASPHYXIATED BABIES
Largely anticipatory.
■ Serial monitoring of parameters such as:
  · Oxygen saturation via pulse oximetry
  · random blood glucose
  · serum electrolytes, urea and creatinine, serum calcium
  · serum troponin
· serum aspartate aminotransferase, serum alanine aminotransferase should be done where facilities are available.

- Derangements should be appropriately corrected according to standard protocols:
  · Prophylactic phenobarbitone is no longer a standard practice
  · Seizures should be managed with slow intravenous phenobarbitone (loading dose of 20mg/kg over 15-20 minutes to be maintained after 24hours with 5-10mg/kg every 30 minutes till a maximum dose of 40mg/kg. The dose should be tailed o ffover many days after serial neurologic physical examination and electroencephalography have consistently shown normal parameters.
  · Fluid restriction in the management of perinatal asphyxia is no longer favored because of the existing risks of renal insufficiency coupled with the uncertain risk of the Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH Secretion. Efforts should be to prevent over-hydration rather than fluid restriction. Administer the exact daily maintenance fluid requirement.
  · Observe for signs of hypovolaemia and hypoperfusion (blood pressure/capillary refill time). Give fluid bolus of normal saline in aliquots of 10 mL/kg if there is circulatory collapse. Use inotropes if hypotension is not corrected by fluid bolus; dobutamine at 5 µg/kg/min preferably, if not available, use dopamine at 5 µg/kg/min and increase as necessary.
  · Serial serum monitoring of glucose is essential, and if there is hypoglycaemia, treat accordingly.
  · Apnea should be managed with frequent airway clearing and ventilation by bag and mask.
  · Nil per oral should be maintained until peristalsis is present and respiratory rate is normal. Feeding should be instituted even when the sucking reflex is still depressed with the use of expressed breast milk administered via a nasogastric or orogastric tube. The volume of milk to be administered will depend on the daily maintenance fluid requirement.
  · Electrolyte derangement should be corrected accordingly
  · There is the need to monitor urine output. Weigh the diaper (1g = 1 mL after noting the weight of dry diaper). The patient can also be catheterized for adequate monitoring of urine output. Invite the nephrologist if oliguria (urine output <1mL/kg/hr) is noted.
  · The use of magnesium sulphate and selective head cooling (if available) improve outcome
  · Oxygen administration as necessary, avoid hypoxaemia. Maintain SpO₂as >94%.

Outcome
- The persistence of abnormal cry and abnormal motor functions beyond the first week of life highly suggests severe cerebral damage.
