SCORPION STING 
Introduction
Scorpion stings occur worldwide. Most cases are minor but significant envenomation resulting to death has been reported. Children are particularly at risk of scorpion stings and envenomation.

CLINICAL FEATURES OF SCORPION STING 
• Local symptom:
  - Localized pains, oedema, erythema, paraesthesia, muscle fasciculation and numbness may occur at the site of sting
• Systemic symptom:
  - Usually due to autonomic discharge: hyper-salivation, profuse diaphoresis, lacrimation, miosis, diarrhoea, vomiting, bradycardia, hypotension, increased respiratory secretions, priapism, tachycardia, hypertension, mydriasis, hyperthermia, hyperglycaemia, agitation, and restlessness may occur.

INVESTIGATIONS OF SCORPION STING 
• There is no specific diagnostic Investigations, and in most cases, no
investigation is required
• Investigations, when required, should be tailored towards the
  complications that may occur

TREATMENT OF SCORPION STING 
Non-drug Treatment
• Allay anxiety
• Cold presses may be applied

Drug Treatment
• Analgesics such as paracetamol or ibuprofen may be used
• Local anaesthetic agent like lidocaine may be infiltrated at the site of bite
• Midazolam or diazepam in addition to allaying anxiety, also act as muscle
  relaxants
• Prazosin for hypertension
• Dobutamine for hypotension
• Nitroglycerin for pulmonary oedema
• Anti-venom are available and increase the rate of toxin elimination
